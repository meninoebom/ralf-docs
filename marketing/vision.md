
## One-Liner

RALF (Responsive Audio Locomotion Framework) — infrastructure for creating mutually transformative engagement between dancers and the scores they create by dancing.

## The Problem RALF Addresses

Western dance architecture typically features:
- An externalized, static score
- Set, static choreography
- Separation of performers from audience (observer vs. observed)

In contrast, African diasporic dance and music traditions (Flamenco, Bomba, Vodun, Samba, Capoeira) are:
- **Dialogical** — dance and music exist in relationship, in conversation
- **Generative** — the interaction itself produces something new
- **Participatory** — no separation of observer and observed; you must participate to comprehend
- **Adaptive and responsive** — flexible, not fixed

RALF brings this dialogical quality into technologically-mediated performance.

## Core Concept: Mutually Transformative Engagement

The dancer's movement shapes the music. The music shapes the dancer's movement. Neither controls the other — they are in dialogue. The outcome is a virtuous cycle: the dancer influences the score and is influenced by it.

Key phrase: "You are live remixing a piece of music with your own movement."

## Weighted Chance as Design Principle

Chance is deliberately built into the system — "a very African thing." An intent might trigger one sound 70% of the time and another 30%. This means the dancer *influences* the score rather than *controlling* it. The unpredictability keeps the dialogue alive and generates novelty in every performance.

## Technologies of Remembering (Broader Framework)

Brandon's broader intellectual framework:
- ~70,000 years ago, humans acquired capacities (coordination, analytical thinking) that enabled dominance but also cause harm — we "dismember" the planet, others, and ourselves
- Art, music, dance, ritual, ceremony co-arose as **technologies of remembering** — practices that reconnect what analytical thinking pulls apart
- These are not static traditions but are **renewed with each generation** — adapted to new circumstances and tools
- Modern dance floors are an updated technology of remembering: "dancing is a technology for remembering yourself, for repair, for coming back to your body after a week of being in your head"
- RALF is the next iteration: using digital tools to create new forms of this ancient practice

## The "Master's Tools" Paradigm

Brandon frames his use of technology through the lens of marginalized communities repurposing tools of power:
- Gay, Black and brown men in 1980s Chicago took drum machines and turntables and created house music — "a new kind of ritual and ceremony of remembering"
- Enslaved Africans took rum barrels (filled with their labor and blood) and transformed them into steel drums — technologies of remembering
- RALF follows this paradigm: taking computational tools and repurposing them for embodied, relational, spiritual practice

## On Technology and AI

Brandon does not 100% believe incorporating technology and AI into dance is good — but feels it is "an urgent thing to explore because of the salience and power of this moment in history."

Analogy: instrument makers are craftspeople and technicians — pianos and guitars are highly precise — "but the spirit that emerges, that they can carry, is so deep, profound, inspirational, timeless. It's possible to take anything and make it into the tool of spirit."

## Music as Highest Knowledge

Reference to Egyptian mystery schools: Pythagoras traveled to Egypt to study; the highest level of mystery was music. Music as conceived by the Egyptians is "the organization of everything" — all matter is ultimately composed of vibrating strings.

## What Doesn't Change

"The technology is shifting under my feet, but the thing I care about does not shift. The compelling part — that inter-relatedness of dancer and music, movement-led composing, the relationality of sound and motion — does not change."
